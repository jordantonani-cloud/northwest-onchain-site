"Tokenization" is one of the most over-pitched words in finance right now, which makes it hard to tell the real opportunity from the hype. Here is what RWA tokenization actually is, in plain terms, and how to judge whether it is worth doing.

## What "RWA tokenization" means

**RWA tokenization is representing ownership of a real-world asset as a transferable token on a blockchain.** The asset stays in the real world — a fund, a bond, a building, a barrel of oil. The token is a legal claim on that asset, recorded on a shared ledger so it can be transferred and settled without the usual chain of intermediaries.

The token is not the asset. It is a wrapper around a legal right to the asset. That distinction matters, because it means the hard work of tokenization is legal and operational, not technical.

## Which assets are being tokenized

Some categories have clear traction because they have an obvious friction to remove:

| Asset class | Why it tokenizes well |
| --- | --- |
| Money-market & treasury funds | Near-instant settlement and 24/7 transferability vs. legacy cutoffs |
| Private credit | Manual servicing and limited secondary liquidity today |
| Bonds | Costly issuance and settlement infrastructure |
| Real estate | High ticket sizes that fractional ownership can open up |
| Commodities | Provenance and transferability across borders |

The pattern is consistent: the assets that benefit most are the ones where **settlement is slow, administration is manual, or distribution is narrow**.

## The real benefits — and the real costs

The benefits, when they apply, are concrete: faster settlement, lower administrative cost, broader distribution, and fractional ownership that opens an asset to more buyers. These are business outcomes a CFO can model.

The costs are equally concrete, and they are where most tokenization projects underestimate the work:

- **Legal structure.** Someone has to make the token a legally enforceable claim on the asset. This is the foundation, and it is not trivial.
- **Custody.** The underlying asset has to be held and the keys managed. Get this wrong and nothing else matters.
- **Compliance.** Securities, KYC/AML, and transfer restrictions usually apply. Compliance is not a feature you add later; it is scope from day one.

Notice that none of the three hardest parts is the smart contract. The code is the easy part. The reason tokenization projects fail is rarely the technology.

## How to tell whether your asset is worth tokenizing

Ask one question: **what specific, costed friction does tokenization remove?**

If the answer is "settlement takes two days and ties up capital," or "administration costs us X per year," or "we can't reach buyers below a $1M ticket," then there is a business case worth sizing. If the answer is "it would be innovative" or "the board wants a tokenization story," there is no case yet — only a cost.

## The bottom line

RWA tokenization is a real tool with real benefits for assets that carry settlement friction, heavy administration, or limited distribution. It is not a default. The decision should start from the friction you are removing and the number attached to it — not from the technology.
