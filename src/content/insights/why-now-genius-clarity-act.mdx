For most of the last decade, the honest reason enterprises didn't build onchain wasn't the technology. It was a lawyer's shrug: *we don't know how this will be regulated.* That answer is changing — and that change, more than any technical breakthrough, is why 2025–26 is the window.

**The short answer:** the GENIUS Act is now law, giving payment stablecoins a federal framework. The CLARITY Act, which would set the broader rules for digital-asset markets, is advancing through the Senate but is not yet law. Together they lift much of the regulatory uncertainty that kept institutional projects on the shelf. The firms that build now will be ready when the rules fully land; the ones that wait will be integrating under pressure.

## What the GENIUS Act did

The GENIUS Act was [signed into law on July 18, 2025](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/07/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-signs-genius-act-into-law/), after passing the Senate 68–30 and clearing the House. It is the first U.S. federal framework for **payment stablecoins**, and its core requirements are straightforward:

- Stablecoins must be backed **one-to-one** by U.S. dollars or other low-risk assets.
- They may only be issued by **permitted issuers** that meet the framework's standards.
- The rules phase in over roughly the next 18 months as regulators finalize implementing regulations.

What changed in practice: stablecoin settlement moved from a gray area into a **sanctioned rail** that a bank or enterprise can build on with a defensible compliance story. That is exactly the shift that makes the payments use case — [taking cost out of the value chain](/insights/how-stablecoins-take-cost-out-of-the-value-chain) — a board-ready conversation rather than a science project.

One caution: "signed into law" is not the same as "fully in force." Rulemaking is ongoing, and the details of who qualifies as a permitted issuer and how reserves are supervised are still being written. Build with that in mind.

## Where the CLARITY Act stands

The CLARITY Act — the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act — is the bigger, harder piece: it would define **market structure**, dividing regulatory oversight of digital assets and reducing the "is this a security?" question that has stalled tokenization and onchain products.

As of June 2026 it is **advancing, not enacted**. It passed the House, and the Senate Banking Committee [approved it on a bipartisan 15–9 vote in May 2026](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/05/14/clarity-act-clears-u-s-senate-committee-on-its-way-to-a-final-test-in-congress). It still has real hurdles: reconciliation with the Senate Agriculture Committee's version, an unresolved ethics provision, and a full Senate floor vote that needs 60 votes. Serious progress — but not a done deal. Anyone telling you market-structure law is settled is ahead of the facts.

## Why this is the window for builders

Regulatory clarity does two things that matter to anyone deciding whether to move:

1. **It unblocks the compliance veto.** The most common reason an onchain project died in committee was risk and legal saying "not until the rules are clear." That objection is weakening.
2. **It rewards being early.** Frameworks reward the institutions already operating when rules take full effect — they shape implementation, secure issuer status, and have live product while competitors are still scoping. Building during the transition is the advantage.

This is not a reason to rush a bad idea onchain. It is a reason to *find the right idea now* and have it built and measured before the field crowds. ## What this doesn't change

It doesn't make blockchain the right answer for every problem — regulation lowering a barrier is not the same as a use case existing. It doesn't replace your own legal counsel; statuses move, and implementing rules are still being written. And it doesn't mean "build fast." It means the cost of waiting just went up.
