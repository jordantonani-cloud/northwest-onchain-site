"Put it onchain" is not a product strategy. But onchain vaults and tokenized credit are quietly becoming the most concrete revenue story in the space — because they let an issuer launch a credit or yield product that is **transparent and composable** in ways a traditional fund structure cannot match.

**The short answer:** an onchain vault is a smart contract that pools capital and runs a defined strategy with real-time, on-chain accounting; tokenized credit puts loans and credit exposure on-chain as transferable tokens. Together they unlock new product lines — yield products, structured credit, collateralized lending — with auditable, real-time reserves. The segment is already material: tokenized private credit is the largest non-Treasury part of the real-world-asset market. The catch is that the risks are real and must be priced, not waved away.

## What is an onchain vault?

A vault is a smart contract that accepts deposits, deploys them to a defined strategy, and accounts for every position and reserve **on-chain, in real time**. Two properties make it more than a wrapper around a fund:

- **Transparency.** An investor can verify what backs their position continuously, rather than trusting a quarterly statement. Reserves are auditable on demand.
- **Composability.** Because the vault is a standard on-chain object, other products can build on top of it — a stablecoin can route idle balances into it, a structured product can use its shares as a building block.

That combination — provable backing plus programmability — is the actual innovation. The yield is secondary.

## What is tokenized credit?

Tokenized credit represents a loan, or a pool of loans, as a transferable on-chain token with its terms embedded in code. Instead of a credit position living in a fund administrator's spreadsheet, it lives as a token that can be distributed programmatically, held with on-chain transparency, and — where regulation and liquidity allow — traded on a secondary market.

For an issuer, that means distribution and servicing become software. For an investor, it means visibility into the collateral and cash flows backing the exposure.

## What new product lines does this unlock?

This is where it gets concrete for a business deciding whether to build:

- **Yield products** — packaging on-chain or tokenized cash-equivalent strategies into a simple, transparent product for fintechs, wallets, or treasuries to embed.
- **Structured credit** — tranching credit exposure with rules enforced by code rather than by a servicer.
- **Collateralized lending** — lending against on-chain or tokenized collateral with real-time, auditable loan-to-value, reducing the opacity that hides risk in traditional private credit.
- **Transparent-reserve funds** — funds whose backing is provable on-chain at any moment, a genuine differentiator for institutional allocators burned by opacity.

## How big is onchain credit today?

Larger than most people assume, and concentrated. Tokenized private credit surpassed [$14 billion in active on-chain loans in 2026](https://blockworks.co/news/private-credit-tokenization-2-trillion-blackrock-maple) and is now the [largest non-Treasury segment of the roughly $34 billion tokenized RWA market](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2026/01/21/private-credit-may-be-the-breakout-use-case-for-tokenization). Reported yields commonly sit in the 8–15% range, and a handful of platforms account for most of the volume.

Now the honest context: the traditional private-credit market is estimated at $3–3.5 trillion. On-chain credit is a fraction of a percent of that — which is exactly why it is an opportunity rather than a crowded trade. The rails work and the early product-market fit is visible; most of the market has not moved yet. ## What are the risks?

A vendor-neutral read has to name them, because the yield headlines rarely do:

- **Smart-contract risk** — a vault is only as safe as its code and its audits.
- **Underwriting risk** — tokenization does not improve a bad loan book; transparent collateral makes risk visible, it does not remove it.
- **Liquidity risk** — "tradeable in principle" is not the same as "liquid when you need to exit."
- **Oracle and dependency risk** — composability cuts both ways; a product is exposed to whatever it builds on.

None of these are reasons not to build. They are reasons to scope carefully, define what "success" and "safe" mean up front, and assemble a team that has shipped this before.
