Most conversations about stablecoins start with the technology. The one that matters to a CFO starts with the invoice: where, exactly, does money leak as a payment moves through your business — and can a stablecoin rail stop the leak?

**The short answer:** stablecoins take cost out of a value chain by collapsing settlement from days to minutes and removing the intermediaries, pre-funding, and float that sit between the payer and the payee. The savings are real and largest in cross-border B2B payments, treasury movement, payroll, and supplier settlement. They are also early — stablecoins are still about 1% of global payment flows — so the work is to model the saving on *your* flow against the cost to integrate it.

## What does "taking cost out of the value chain" actually mean?

A traditional cross-border payment is not one transaction. It is a relay: your bank, one or more correspondent banks, an FX desk, and the recipient's bank, each taking a fee, a spread, or a day. To make it work, money sits **pre-funded** in accounts around the world, and value is lost to **float** while the payment clears over two or three days.

A stablecoin payment is a single transfer of the same asset between two wallets. It settles in minutes, 24/7, with no batch windows and no relay of intermediaries. The cost that disappears is not just a transaction fee — it is the layered intermediary margin, the FX spread, the capital locked in pre-funding, and the float. That is what "taking cost out of the value chain" means: fewer hands, less trapped capital, faster finality.

## Where stablecoins save the most money today

Not everywhere equally. The savings concentrate where the traditional rail is slowest and most intermediated:

- **Cross-border B2B settlement** — paying overseas suppliers, contractors, or subsidiaries, where correspondent banking is slow and the FX spread is the real cost.
- **Treasury movement** — sweeping cash between entities and geographies without waiting on cut-off times or pre-funding every corridor.
- **Payroll and contractor payouts** — paying a global workforce on the same day, in a dollar-stable unit, without per-corridor banking overhead.
- **Supplier and marketplace settlement** — paying out continuously instead of in daily batches, improving working capital on both sides.

The common thread: a flow that today crosses borders, time zones, or banking cut-offs. Domestic instant payments that already clear cheaply are usually not where stablecoins win.

## How big is this, really?

Big enough to take seriously, early enough to be a real edge. Stablecoin supply rose to roughly [$316 billion by April 2026, up 54% in fifteen months](https://www.plasma.to/learn/stablecoin-transaction-volume), and on-chain transfer volume ran into the tens of trillions in 2025. Institutional rails are live: Visa's stablecoin settlement program reached a multi-billion-dollar annualized run rate by early 2026.

But keep it honest. Stablecoins remain only about [1% of global payment flows](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/stablecoin-utility-future-of-payments/), and a great deal of on-chain volume is trading, not payments. The trajectory is what makes this a "build now" story: cross-border B2B stablecoin payments are [projected to reach roughly $5 trillion by 2035](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2026/04/27/cross-border-b2b-stablecoin-payments-to-rise-by-over-37-000-to-usd5t-by-2035) from a small base today. The institutions that wire the rails in now are positioned for that curve; the ones that wait will integrate under pressure.

## When stablecoins are the wrong tool

A vendor-neutral advisor has to be willing to say it: sometimes your existing rail is fine. If a payment already clears instantly and cheaply, a stablecoin adds integration and compliance work for little gain. If your real bottleneck is counterparty readiness, accounting treatment, or internal controls rather than settlement speed, fix that first. The point is never "use a stablecoin." It is "remove the cost that is actually there."
