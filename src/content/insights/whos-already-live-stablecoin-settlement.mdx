When a crypto startup adopts stablecoins, it proves nothing. When **Visa, Stripe, and Mastercard** rebuild their settlement rails on them, it proves the thesis. That is what happened over 2025 and 2026 — and it is the clearest signal yet that stablecoin settlement has moved from experiment to infrastructure.

**The short version:** the companies that own global payments are already live. Below is what each has actually shipped, what the onchain version changed, and the question it should raise for your own business.

## Who's already doing it

**Visa — settlement in production, at scale.** Visa [launched USDC settlement in the U.S. in late 2025](https://investor.visa.com/news/news-details/2025/Visa-Launches-Stablecoin-Settlement-in-the-United-States-Marking-a-Breakthrough-for-Stablecoin-Integration/default.aspx), with partner banks settling over the Solana network, and by April 2026 its stablecoin settlement program [reached roughly a $7 billion annualized run rate](https://www.pymnts.com/cryptocurrency/2026/mastercard-and-visa-back-stealth-stablecoin-platform/) — up about 50% in a single quarter — spanning nine blockchains and more than 130 stablecoin-linked card programs across 50-plus countries. This is not a pilot; it is a growing settlement business.

**Stripe — a $1.1 billion bet on the rails.** Stripe acquired the stablecoin platform **Bridge** for roughly $1.1 billion in early 2025, and by 2026 reported stablecoin payment volume of around $400 billion, with an estimated 60% in B2B flows. Bridge-enabled stablecoin cards went live across 18 countries with plans for 100-plus. Stripe is wiring stablecoins into the default payment stack for millions of businesses.

**Mastercard — buying the infrastructure.** In March 2026, Mastercard [agreed to acquire stablecoin infrastructure firm BVNK for up to $1.8 billion](https://www.pymnts.com/cryptocurrency/2026/mastercard-and-visa-back-stealth-stablecoin-platform/) (pending regulatory close), to connect onchain stablecoin payments to its global fiat rails for cross-border, remittance, B2B, and 24/7 settlement.

## What the onchain version actually changed

Every one of these moves does the same thing: it replaces a multi-day relay of correspondent banks, FX desks, and pre-funded accounts with a single transfer that settles in minutes, around the clock. The cost removed isn't just a transaction fee — it's the layered intermediary margin, the FX spread, the capital trapped in pre-funding, and the float. We break down the mechanics in [How stablecoins take cost out of the value chain](/insights/how-stablecoins-take-cost-out-of-the-value-chain).

It is also still early: stablecoins remain only about 1% of global payment flows. The infrastructure is built; the migration has barely begun.

## What this could mean for your business

If the companies that *are* the payment system are rebuilding settlement on stablecoins, the relevant question stops being "is this real?" and becomes "where in our flows would this matter?" Cross-border supplier payments, treasury movement between entities, global payroll, marketplace payouts — anywhere money today crosses a border or a banking cut-off is a candidate.

That doesn't mean every business should move. The honest answer is sometimes "your existing rail is fine." But the firms that understand where stablecoin settlement fits *now* — while it's still a 1% market — are the ones who will have it running while competitors are still scoping.
