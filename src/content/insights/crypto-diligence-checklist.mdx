If you are a VC or family office being pitched crypto, tokenization, or RWA deals, the core discipline has not changed: **read the contract and the cap table before you wire money.** What changes is that "the contract" now includes a smart contract, and the cap table includes a token. Here is a checklist that covers both.

## 1. Team and track record

Start where you always start. Has this team shipped before? Are the people handling real value identifiable and accountable, or anonymous? Are incentives aligned, or does the team win whether or not investors do?

Anonymity is not automatically disqualifying in crypto, but anonymity **plus** custody of real value is a different risk than a doxxed founder with a reputation to protect. Weigh it accordingly.

## 2. Token design and supply

This is the part with no traditional analogue, and it is where a lot of value quietly leaks. Work through:

| Question | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| What is the total and circulating supply? | Dilution and real vs. headline valuation |
| Who holds insider allocations, and what is the vesting? | Short or no vesting = sell pressure on you |
| When do major unlocks happen? | Unlock cliffs move price regardless of fundamentals |
| Who can mint new tokens or change the rules? | Concentrated control is concentrated risk |

A beautiful product attached to a token that insiders can dump in three months is not a good investment. The token design is part of the deal, not a detail.

## 3. Technical risk

You do not need to audit the code yourself, but someone on your side needs to read it. Confirm:

- **Audits.** Have the contracts been audited by a credible firm, and were the findings fixed? An audit is necessary, not sufficient.
- **Custody and keys.** How is value held, and who controls the keys? Key management is the most common point of catastrophic failure.
- **Upgrade controls.** Can the contracts be changed after you invest, and by whom? An upgradeable contract with one admin key is a trapdoor.

## 4. Legal structure

Ask the deceptively simple question: **what is this token, legally, and what does it entitle me to?** Some tokens are claims on cash flows or assets. Some are governance rights. Many are claims on nothing at all. The legal wrapper determines whether you own something or merely hold something.

For tokenization and RWA deals specifically, trace the link from the token to the underlying asset and confirm it is legally enforceable. A token that "represents" an asset with no enforceable claim behind it represents nothing.

## 5. Red flags that should stop a wire

Any one of these warrants pausing to understand it fully before capital moves:

- An anonymous team with custody of real value.
- Large insider allocations with short or no vesting.
- Unaudited or freely upgradeable contracts with concentrated admin control.
- A token with no clear legal claim on anything.

None of these is automatically fatal, but each is a place where money disappears, and "we didn't look closely" is not a defense you want to give your LPs.

## The bottom line

Crypto diligence is traditional diligence plus token design, technical risk, and legal structure. The investors who lose money in this space are rarely the ones who asked too many questions. If a deal cannot survive this checklist, that is the checklist doing its job.
