If you have a board mandate to "do something with blockchain," the first question your CFO will ask is: **what will a proof of concept cost, and what do we get for it?** Here is a straight answer, with the ranges and the reasoning behind them.

## What a blockchain proof of concept actually costs

There are two costs, and conflating them is where budgets go wrong. First is the cost to **decide what to build** — diagnosis and scoping. Second is the cost to **build the thing** — the PoC itself.

| Phase | What you get | Typical cost | Timeline |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Opportunity Audit | A fast read on whether a real onchain opportunity exists and how big | $1,500 flat | 3–5 days |
| Diagnostic Sprint | Defined opportunity, success metrics, costed roadmap, the right team | $4,000–$7,500 | 1–2 weeks |
| PoC build | A working proof of concept run to the agreed metrics | $15,000–$75,000+ | 4–10 weeks |

These ranges are Northwest Onchain's framing based on how enterprise PoCs are actually scoped — not a vendor quote. The build figure moves with three things: how many systems you integrate, whether you need custody and key management, and how much compliance overhead the use case carries.

## Why the diagnosis comes first (and costs less than the build)

The most expensive blockchain project is the one that should never have been built. Before anyone writes a contract, you want an honest read on whether onchain is the right tool at all. That is what the [Opportunity Audit](/services#audit) and the [Diagnostic Sprint](/services#sprint) are for: they exist to define the opportunity and, just as often, to **kill the bad idea cheaply**.

A paid diagnosis also qualifies the work. Anyone unwilling to spend $1,500 to find out whether there is a real opportunity was never going to fund a $50,000 build. The fee credits toward whatever comes next, so it costs nothing if you proceed.

## What drives the build number up or down

Three factors explain most of the variance in PoC build cost:

- **Integrations.** A PoC that reads and writes to one internal system is cheap. One that touches your core ledger, a KYC provider, and a custody partner is not.
- **Custody and keys.** The moment real value or real assets are involved, key management and custody design become the hard part — and the expensive part.
- **Compliance surface.** A loyalty-points PoC carries almost no regulatory weight. A tokenized-fund PoC carries a lot. Compliance scoping is work, and it belongs in the budget from day one.

## How to scope a PoC so the budget maps to an outcome

A proof of concept should prove or kill **one specific, measurable claim**. "Explore tokenization" is not a PoC; it is a way to spend money. "Cut settlement from two days to under five minutes for this instrument, measured across 100 test transactions" is a PoC. The metric is the scope, and the scope is the budget.

This is the discipline behind [our process](/process): success criteria, cost, and ROI are defined before a line of code is written. If we cannot agree what winning looks like, we do not start — because at that point, no price is cheap.

## The bottom line

Budget roughly **$15k–$75k for a build** and **$4k–$7.5k for the scoping** that should precede it, and expect **four to ten weeks** once metrics are agreed. But treat the number as secondary. The real cost control is a PoC tied to a single measurable outcome, run by a team that is willing to tell you when the answer is "don't."
